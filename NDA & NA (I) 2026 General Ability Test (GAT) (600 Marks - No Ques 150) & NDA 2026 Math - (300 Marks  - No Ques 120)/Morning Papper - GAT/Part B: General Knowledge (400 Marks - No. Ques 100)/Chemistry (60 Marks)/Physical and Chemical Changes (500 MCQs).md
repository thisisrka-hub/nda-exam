# NDA 2026 Chemistry: Physical and Chemical Changes (500 MCQs)

## Important Notes and Formulas

### Physical Changes
- Physical changes are changes in which the composition of matter remains the same, but physical properties change.
- Examples: melting, boiling, freezing, condensation, evaporation, crushing, bending, cutting, dissolving.
- A physical change is reversible in many cases.
- No new substance is formed in a physical change.
- Physical properties include color, odor, density, melting point, boiling point, hardness, etc.

### Chemical Changes
- Chemical changes are changes in which new substances are formed with different chemical composition.
- A chemical reaction occurs; atoms rearrange to form new molecules.
- Examples: burning, rusting, decomposition, fermentation, neutralization.
- Chemical changes are usually irreversible.
- Energy is released or absorbed during chemical reactions.
- Chemical changes involve breaking and forming of chemical bonds.

### Elements
- An element is a pure substance consisting of atoms of only one type.
- Elements have characteristic properties (e.g., iron is magnetic, sulfur is yellow).
- There are about 118 known elements, arranged in the periodic table.
- Elements exist as atoms, molecules, or in crystal structures.
- Chemical symbols represent elements: H for hydrogen, O for oxygen, C for carbon, etc.

### Mixtures
- A mixture is a combination of two or more substances that are not chemically combined.
- Constituents retain their individual properties in a mixture.
- Mixtures can be homogeneous (uniform composition) or heterogeneous (non-uniform).
- Examples: salt water, sand and water, air, brass, steel.
- Mixtures can be separated by physical methods: evaporation, filtration, distillation, etc.

### Compounds
- A compound is a pure substance formed by chemical combination of two or more elements.
- A compound has fixed composition and definite properties different from its elements.
- Examples: water (H2O), salt (NaCl), sugar (C12H22O11).
- Compounds are represented by chemical formulae.
- Compounds can be decomposed into elements by chemical reactions.

### Chemical Symbols and Formulae
- Chemical symbols are one or two-letter abbreviations for elements.
- First letter is uppercase; second letter (if any) is lowercase: e.g., N for nitrogen, Na for sodium, Cl for chlorine.
- Atomic mass unit (amu) is 1/12 of mass of carbon-12 atom.
- Molar mass is mass of one mole of substance in grams.
- Chemical formulae show the types and number of atoms in a molecule or compound.
- Example: H2O has 2 hydrogen atoms and 1 oxygen atom.

### Chemical Equations
- Chemical equations represent chemical reactions using symbols and formulae.
- Reactants are on the left; products are on the right.
- The equation must be balanced: same number of atoms of each type on both sides.
- Example: 2H2 + O2 → 2H2O (balanced).
- Coefficients show the ratio of molecules/moles involved in the reaction.
- States of matter are shown: (s) solid, (l) liquid, (g) gas, (aq) aqueous solution.

### Law of Chemical Combination
- Law of Conservation of Mass: Mass is neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction.
- Law of Definite Proportions: A compound always contains the same elements in the same proportion by mass.
- Law of Multiple Proportions: When two elements form more than one compound, the masses of one element that combine with a fixed mass of the other are in simple whole number ratios.

### Properties of Air
- Air is a mixture of 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, and 1% other gases (argon, CO2, etc.).
- Air supports combustion (due to oxygen).
- Air is essential for respiration.
- Oxygen reacts with elements to form oxides (e.g., 2Mg + O2 → 2MgO).
- Air is colorless and odorless.
- Air is compressible and has mass.
- Nitrogen is relatively inert and is used in many applications.

### Properties of Water
- Water (H2O) is composed of hydrogen and oxygen in a 2:1 mass ratio.
- Water is a universal solvent; it dissolves many substances.
- Pure water boils at 100°C and freezes at 0°C (at 1 atm pressure).
- Water has a density of 1 g/cm³ or 1000 kg/m³ at 4°C.
- Water supports most forms of life.
- Water undergoes physical changes: melting, boiling, evaporation, condensation, sublimation.
- Water undergoes chemical reactions: combustion of hydrogen, reaction with metals, decomposition by electric current.
