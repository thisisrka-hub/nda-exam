# NDA 2026 Chemistry: Chemical Changes, Elements, Mixtures, Compounds, Laws (1000 MCQs)

## Important Notes and Formulas

### Physical Changes
- Definition: Changes in physical properties without forming new substances.
- Examples: Melting, freezing, boiling, evaporation, condensation, dissolution, crystallization.
- Characteristics: Reversible; no new substance formed; no new color, odor, or state change produces new compound.
- Energy: Usually requires less energy than chemical changes.
- Properties affected: State, color (appearance), shape, size, density, but not composition.

### Chemical Changes
- Definition: Changes where new substances are formed; bonds are broken/formed.
- Examples: Burning, rusting, cooking, digestion, fermentation, respiration.
- Characteristics: Usually irreversible; new substance with new properties formed.
- Energy: Absorbed (endothermic) or released (exothermic).
- Indicators: Temperature change, gas formation, color change, light emission, sound, precipitate formation.
- Chemical equations represent these changes: Reactants → Products.

### Elements
- Definition: Pure substances made of only one type of atom.
- Cannot be broken down into simpler substances by physical or chemical means.
- Examples: Fe, O2, N2, H2, Cu, Au, Cl2, S.
- Symbol: One or two-letter notation (H=Hydrogen, He=Helium, Cl=Chlorine).
- Classification: Metals, non-metals, metalloids.
- Atomic number: Number of protons (unique for each element).

### Mixtures
- Definition: Two or more elements or compounds mixed but not chemically bonded.
- Characteristics: Retains properties of components; composition varies; can be separated.
- Types: Homogeneous (uniform, e.g., salt solution) and heterogeneous (non-uniform, e.g., sand+water).
- Examples: Air, seawater, concrete, soil, alloys.
- Separation: Filtration, evaporation, distillation, magnetic separation, chromatography.

### Compounds
- Definition: Substances formed by chemical combination of two or more elements in fixed ratio.
- Characteristics: Fixed composition; has new properties different from elements; cannot separate by physical means.
- Examples: H2O, CO2, NaCl, CaCO3, NH3, HCl.
- Molecular formula: Shows type and number of each atom (H2O = 2 H + 1 O).
- Empirical formula: Simplest whole number ratio of atoms.

### Symbols and Symbols of Elements
- Symbol: 1-2 letter notation (capital first letter, lowercase second if applicable).
- Examples: H (Hydrogen), He (Helium), Li (Lithium), Be (Beryllium), C (Carbon), N (Nitrogen), O (Oxygen), F (Fluorine), Ne (Neon), Na (Sodium), Mg (Magnesium), Al (Aluminum), Si (Silicon), P (Phosphorus), S (Sulfur), Cl (Chlorine), Ar (Argon), K (Potassium), Ca (Calcium), Fe (Iron), Cu (Copper), Zn (Zinc), Ag (Silver), Au (Gold), Pb (Lead), Hg (Mercury).

### Chemical Formulae
- Formula: Represents composition of a compound using element symbols and subscripts.
- Subscript: Number of atoms of that element in one molecule.
- Rules: Valency determines subscripts; total positive and negative charges must balance.
- Examples: H2O (water), NaCl (salt), CaSO4 (calcium sulfate), Al2O3 (aluminum oxide).
- Reading: NaCl = 1 Na + 1 Cl; Ca(OH)2 = 1 Ca + 2 O + 2 H.

### Chemical Equations
- Equation: Symbolic representation showing reactants → products.
- Unbalanced equation: Shows reactants and products but atoms not balanced.
- Balanced equation: Same number of atoms of each element on both sides.
- Methods: Hit and trial (try numbers until balanced), algebraic method.
- Example: Unbalanced: Fe + O2 → Fe2O3; Balanced: 4Fe + 3O2 → 2Fe2O3.
- Coefficients: Numbers in front of formulas to balance; do not change formulas.

### Law of Chemical Combination

**Law of Conservation of Mass:**
- Total mass of reactants = Total mass of products.
- Matter cannot be created or destroyed in a chemical reaction.

**Law of Constant Proportions (Definite Proportions):**
- A pure compound always contains the same elements in the same proportion by mass.
- Example: H2O always has H and O in 1:8 mass ratio.

**Law of Multiple Proportions:**
- If two elements combine to form multiple compounds, the ratios of masses of one element combining with fixed mass of other are in simple whole numbers.
- Example: CO (carbon monoxide), CO2 (carbon dioxide); ratio of oxygen is 1:2.

**Law of Reciprocal Proportions:**
- When two elements combine separately with a third element, the ratio in which they combine with third element is simple or reciprocal of their ratio when combining with each other.

### Properties of Air
- Composition: N2 (78%), O2 (21%), Ar (0.93%), CO2 (0.04%), other gases (0.03%).
- Density: Approximately 1.29 kg/m³ at sea level (denser than any gas individually).
- Pressure: Atmospheric pressure = 760 mm Hg = 101.3 kPa.
- Supports combustion: Due to oxygen content.
- Supports life: Contains oxygen for respiration.
- Movement: Wind is moving air (caused by pressure and temperature differences).
- Properties: Transparent, colorless, odorless, can be liquefied at low temp and high pressure.
- Uses: Respiration, combustion, power for windmills, lifting balloons with hot air.

### Properties of Water
- Formula: H2O; Molecular weight: 18 g/mol.
- Physical properties: Colorless, odorless, tasteless liquid; mp 0°C; bp 100°C.
- Freezing point: 0°C; water expands on freezing (unusual property).
- Density: Maximum at 4°C; less dense as solid (ice floats).
- Solvent: Universal solvent; dissolves most substances.
- Chemical properties: Can be decomposed by electrolysis: 2H2O → 2H2 + O2.
- Oxidizing and reducing agent: Depending on conditions.
- Hardness: Caused by salts of Ca2+ and Mg2+ (temporary and permanent).
- Purity: Distilled water is pure water (H2O only).
- Uses: Drinking, agriculture, cooling, cleaning, chemical reactions.
- Hydrated salts: Contain water molecules (e.g., CuSO4·5H2O).
