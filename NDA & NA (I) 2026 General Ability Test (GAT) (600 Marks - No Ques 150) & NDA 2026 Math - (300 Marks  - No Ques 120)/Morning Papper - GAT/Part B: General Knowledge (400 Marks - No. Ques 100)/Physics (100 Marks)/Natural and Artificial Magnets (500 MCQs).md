# NDA 2026 Physics: Natural and Artificial Magnets (500 MCQs)

## Important Notes and Formulas

### Natural and Artificial Magnets
- A magnet is a material or object that produces a magnetic field.
- Natural magnets are found in nature, like lodestone. Artificial magnets are made by humans, such as bar magnets and electromagnets.
- Permanent magnets retain their magnetism for a long time. Temporary magnets lose it when the external field is removed.
- Magnetic poles: every magnet has two poles, north (N) and south (S).
- Like poles repel; unlike poles attract.
- A magnet always has both poles; magnetic monopoles have not been observed.
- Magnetic field lines emerge from the north pole and enter the south pole.
- The strength of a magnetic field is measured in tesla (T) or gauss (1 T = 10^4 gauss).

### Properties of a Magnet
- Magnets attract magnetic materials such as iron, nickel, and cobalt.
- Magnets do not attract non-magnetic materials like wood or plastic.
- The magnetic field is strongest near the poles.
- A freely suspended magnet aligns itself along the north-south direction.
- Magnetic field lines never intersect.
- A magnetic field can be mapped with iron filings or a compass.
- Heating a magnet or hammering it can demagnetize it.
- Magnetic induction: a magnetic material can become magnetized when placed in a magnetic field.

### Earth as a Magnet
- Earth behaves like a giant magnet with a magnetic north pole and a magnetic south pole.
- The geographic north pole is near the magnetic south pole, so Earth's magnetic north pole points toward geographic north.
- Magnetic declination is the angle between geographic north and magnetic north.
- Magnetic inclination (dip) is the angle made by Earth's magnetic field with the horizontal.
- The magnetic field of Earth protects us from charged particles from the sun.
- The compass needle points approximately to Earth's magnetic north.
- Earth's magnetic poles slowly shift over time.

### Key Concepts and Formulas
- Right-hand thumb rule: for a current-carrying conductor, the direction of the magnetic field is given by curling the fingers around the conductor with the thumb pointing in the current direction.
- Fleming’s left-hand rule for motors: thumb = force, first finger = field, second finger = current.
- Magnetic field near a bar magnet is similar to the field of a dipole.
- Magnetic flux Φ = B A cos θ, where B is magnetic field, A is area, and θ is angle between field and normal.
